Koi Keeping

Koi Fish Guide: Varieties, Care & Stocking for SoCal Ponds

By Pacific Coast Ponds · 13 min read · Updated 2025

Koi (Cyprinus rubrofuscus) are among the most beautiful and long-lived fish in the world — they regularly live 25 to 35 years and can grow to 36 inches in optimal conditions. Southern California's mild winters and warm summers create a uniquely favorable environment for koi, but also some unique challenges. This guide covers everything you need to know about selecting, stocking, and caring for koi in a SoCal pond.

Koi Varieties: The Major Classifications

The world of koi is organized by variety — defined by color pattern, scale type, and body conformation. In Japan, where modern koi were developed in the Niigata Prefecture region in the 19th century, variety classification is a precise discipline governed by organizations including the Zen Nippon Airinkai (ZNA) and the All Japan Koi Show. For most pond owners in Southern California, understanding the major varieties helps in selecting fish that will be visually striking and complementary in a group setting.

Variety Pattern Colors Notes for Beginners
Kohaku White base with red (hi) markings White and red/orange-red The most popular koi variety worldwide. Clean, high-contrast markings make it visually striking in any pond. Hardy and widely available. Excellent first koi.
Taisho Sanshoku (Sanke) White base with red markings and black (sumi) spots White, red, and black One of the "Big Three" varieties. Sumi appears as discrete spots, not as a body base color. Elegant and classic. Good for intermediate keepers — sumi pattern evolves as the fish grows.
Showa Sanshoku (Showa) Black base with red and white markings Black, red, and white Distinguished from Sanke by the black base color. Dramatic, bold appearance. Sumi wraps around the body. One of the Big Three — highly prized in competition.
Asagi Blue-grey reticulated scale pattern on back, red/orange on belly and fins Blue-grey and orange-red One of the oldest koi varieties. Understated and elegant. The reticulated (net-like) scale pattern is unique. Color deepens with age.
Ogon Single solid metallic color, no pattern Gold (Yamabuki Ogon), platinum/silver (Platinum Ogon), orange Highly reflective metallic scales make these fish catch light dramatically. Simple, bold presence in any pond. Hardy and easy to keep. Excellent beginner koi.
Utsuri Black base with one secondary color in a lacquer-like pattern Black and white (Shiro Utsuri), black and red (Hi Utsuri), black and yellow (Ki Utsuri) High contrast and bold. Shiro Utsuri (black and white) is the most popular. Pattern develops and refines with age. Dramatic visual impact.
Bekko White, red, or yellow base with black (sumi) spots White/red/yellow base with black spots Think of Bekko as the inverse of Utsuri. Clean, spotted appearance. Less common but striking. Shiro Bekko (white with black spots) is most frequently seen.
Goromo Kohaku base pattern with blue-edged scales over the hi (red) areas White, red, and blue-grey edge A Kohaku with an additional overlay of blue-grey scale edging. Refined and complex appearance. Highly valued by collectors. Pattern continues to develop for years.
Chagoi Solid or reticulated brown/tea color Brown, olive, tea, copper Not flashy, but extraordinarily valuable in a pond: Chagoi are the friendliest and most personable koi, often taming quickly and eating from the hand. They also tend to grow the largest of all varieties — 28"+ in good conditions. Highly recommended.
Tancho Pure white body with a single circular red spot on the head only White with red spot Not a separate variety but a classification that can appear in Kohaku, Sanke, or Showa. Named for the Japanese red-crowned crane. The most Japanese of aesthetics — minimalist and highly prized.

Recommendations for First-Time Koi Owners

If you are selecting koi for your first pond, we recommend starting with three varieties that combine visual impact with ease of care: Kohaku (clean white-and-red contrast), Ogon (dramatic metallic flash), and Chagoi (personality, friendliness, and impressive growth). Together they create a visually diverse and behaviorally interesting pond. Add a Shiro Utsuri for bold black-and-white contrast if you want a fourth variety.

How to Buy Healthy Koi

The quality of the fish you start with has a significant impact on your long-term success and enjoyment. A sickly koi purchased from a poor source can introduce disease to your entire pond, wiping out a collection you have spent years building. Understanding where to buy and what to look for is essential knowledge for every koi keeper.

Where to Buy

Local koi specialty dealers are the preferred source for most buyers. These dealers keep fish in purpose-built koi facilities with proper filtration, maintain stable populations rather than constantly rotating stock, and can provide detailed information about each fish's origin, age, and health history. Several excellent koi dealers operate in Southern California — ask your pond builder or fellow koi keepers for local recommendations.

General pet stores (including the pond sections of big-box retailers) sell koi, but the quality is highly variable. Fish are typically young, small, and of unknown lineage. Water quality in retail tanks is often poor. Fish from these sources tend to carry higher disease loads due to stress and overcrowding. If you purchase from a pet store, quarantine is non-negotiable.

Online specialty importers offer access to higher-grade Japanese koi that may not be available locally. Reputable online importers ship via overnight air freight with oxygen-infused bags and receive high-quality fish directly from Japan. The risk is that you cannot assess the fish personally before purchase. Work only with established importers who have strong reviews and clear health guarantees.

Japan direct imports — purchasing koi directly at Japanese koi auctions or from Niigata breeders — is the domain of serious collectors and is typically reserved for show-grade fish worth thousands of dollars per specimen.

What to Look for in a Healthy Koi

When evaluating a koi for purchase, observe it in the tank for at least five minutes before making any decision:

  • Active, level swimming. A healthy koi swims throughout the tank at a normal depth — not listing to one side, not holding near the surface, not resting on the bottom.
  • Erect fins. All fins — dorsal, pectoral, pelvic, anal, caudal — should be fully extended during active swimming. Clamped fins pressed against the body are a classic stress or disease indicator.
  • Clean body surface. No white spots (ich), no raised scales (dropsy), no red sores or ulcers, no visible parasites or abnormal growths.
  • Clear eyes. Eyes should be clear and symmetrically placed. Bulging, cloudy, or sunken eyes indicate illness.
  • Body symmetry. View the fish from above if possible. The body should be straight without any curvature. A kinked or twisted spine cannot be corrected.
  • Active feeding response. If the dealer feeds the fish while you watch, a healthy koi should approach and compete for food energetically.

Quarantine: Non-Negotiable

Every new koi — regardless of where it was purchased or how healthy it appears — must be quarantined in a separate tank for a minimum of three to four weeks before being introduced to your main pond. This is not optional. A single new fish carrying Koi Herpesvirus (KHV), velvet, flukes, or bacterial disease can devastate an established collection. Quarantine tanks do not need to be elaborate: a 100- to 300-gallon stock tank with a sponge filter and a small UV clarifier is sufficient. Monitor the fish daily and treat any issues in the quarantine tank before introduction.

Transporting and Introducing New Koi

When bringing new koi home, minimize time in the transport bag — two to four hours maximum in warm weather. Float the sealed bag in the pond for 20 to 30 minutes before opening to equalize water temperature gradually. After temperature equalization, open the bag and allow pond water to mix in over several minutes before releasing the fish. Do not pour the bag water directly into your pond — it may carry disease organisms from the seller's facility.

Stocking Your Pond: Size and Density

The fundamental rule of koi stocking is 250 gallons per 12 inches of koi. This conservative benchmark accounts for normal filtration and leaves a safety margin for growth and unexpected variables. For a full breakdown of stocking calculations by pond size, see our dedicated koi pond size guide.

Introducing Fish to a New Pond

Do not add all your koi at once when stocking a new pond. A new pond's biological filter needs time to establish bacteria colonies capable of handling your full fish load. Add fish in groups of two to four over a period of four to six weeks, testing water for ammonia and nitrite between each addition. This gradual approach allows the biofilter to catch up with each incremental increase in waste load.

After any addition of new fish, monitor ammonia daily for two to four weeks. A small ammonia spike after adding fish is normal and expected; the spike should resolve within a few days as the biological filter responds. If ammonia climbs above 0.5 ppm and stays elevated, reduce feeding immediately, perform a partial water change, and consider whether you have added too many fish too quickly.

SoCal Stocking Considerations

In Southern California's mild climate, koi metabolism and waste production remain active throughout the year. There is no winter dormancy period to give your pond and filter a seasonal rest. This means the cumulative waste load on your pond is higher per year than in cold-climate ponds, even at the same stocking density. The practical implication: stock at the conservative end of the stocking guideline for your first year, monitor water quality through at least one full summer cycle, and only increase fish numbers once you have confirmed that your filtration can handle the load without ammonia or nitrite spikes.

Feeding Koi in Southern California

Feeding is the area where most beginner koi owners make their first and most damaging mistake: overfeeding. Uneaten food is the single largest source of ammonia in most backyard ponds, and it only takes a few minutes for uneaten food to begin breaking down in the water column.

The Five-Minute Rule

Feed only what your koi can consume in five minutes. After five minutes, remove any uneaten food with a net before it sinks and begins to decompose. This rule is non-negotiable. It is far better to underfeed slightly than to overfeed and suffer an ammonia spike that stresses or kills your fish.

Feeding Schedule by Season

Unlike cold-climate ponds where koi stop eating during winter dormancy, Southern California koi remain active and require feeding year-round. However, feeding frequency and food type should be adjusted with water temperature:

  • Summer (water temp above 70°F): Feed two to three times daily with a high-quality, high-protein growth formula. Koi metabolism is at its peak and they will benefit from frequent, nutritious feeding. Color-enhancing foods containing spirulina or krill carotenoids can be incorporated in summer for maximum color development.
  • Spring and fall (60–70°F): Feed once or twice daily. Reduce portion sizes slightly as metabolism begins to slow. Transition from high-protein food toward a lower-protein, wheat-germ-based formula as temperatures fall.
  • Cooler months (below 60°F): Switch entirely to wheat germ-based food — it is easier to digest in cold water and produces less waste. In OC/LA/SD, water temperatures below 55°F are rare, but if they occur, feed once daily at most and reduce portions significantly.

Food Quality Matters

Cheap koi food is a false economy. Low-quality food is less digestible, meaning more of it passes through the fish as waste — directly increasing your ammonia load. High-quality koi food has better protein profiles, higher digestibility rates, and produces less waste per feeding. Look for food with a protein content of 32 to 38 percent for growth seasons, formulated specifically for koi (not generic "pond fish" food). Premium brands include Hikari, Saki-Hikari, Mazuri, and Dainichi — all widely available in SoCal from specialty dealers and online retailers.

Common Koi Health Issues

The most powerful disease prevention strategy available to any koi keeper is maintaining excellent water quality. Koi in clean, well-oxygenated, properly buffered water with stable parameters have robust immune systems and resist most pathogens that opportunistically infect stressed fish. The majority of koi disease outbreaks are either directly caused by poor water quality or significantly exacerbated by it. Test your water before treating your fish.

Disease / Issue Symptoms Treatment
White Spot (Ich)
Ichthyophthirius multifiliis		 Fine white spots (like salt grains) across fins and body; scratching against rocks; lethargy in severe cases Raise water temperature gradually to 82–84°F (accelerates parasite life cycle); add non-iodized salt at 0.3%; treat with proprietary ich treatment. Usually resolves in 7–14 days. Quarantine affected fish if possible.
Anchor Worm
Lernaea		 Visible elongated parasites (resembling threads or pins) embedded in scales; red inflamed areas at attachment points; fish flashing (scratching) against surfaces Physical removal with forceps (grip at base, pull straight out); treat affected area with topical antiseptic; treat entire pond with diflubenzuron or potassium permanganate to eliminate larvae in the water column. Repeat in 10–14 days to catch second generation.
Fin Rot
Bacterial (Aeromonas, Pseudomonas)		 Ragged, fraying, or discolored fin edges; fin material dissolving progressively; occasionally red streaking near the body Address water quality first — fin rot almost always follows poor water conditions. Perform partial water change. Treat with antibiotic bath (Maracyn-Two or similar); topical antiseptic on affected fins. Quarantine severely affected fish.
Dropsy
Multifactorial — often bacterial		 Raised, pinecone-like scales (osmotic failure causing fluid accumulation); bloated body; lethargic behavior; loss of appetite Dropsy indicates severe internal organ failure. Quarantine immediately. Treatment success rate is low — antibiotic baths and Epsom salt may help in early stages. Euthanize humanely if the fish does not respond within a week, to prevent suffering and protect tank mates.
Ulcers
Bacterial (Aeromonas hydrophila)		 Open red sores or craters in the flesh; often preceded by a small red lesion; secondary fungal infection (white cotton-like growth) can develop around ulcers Clean affected area with diluted iodine or Betadine; apply topical wound dressing (Koi wound/ulcer treatment paste); address underlying water quality issue. Medicated food (Medikoi) can help with internal bacterial infection. In severe cases, antibiotic injection by a veterinarian is most effective.
Koi Herpesvirus (KHV)
Cyprinid herpesvirus 3		 Rapid onset mass mortality; gill necrosis; sunken eyes; lethargy; often strikes when water temperatures are in the 60–77°F range No cure. KHV is a notifiable disease in California — you may be required to report outbreaks to CDFW. Quarantine all surviving fish. Depopulate affected pond and thoroughly disinfect before restocking. Prevention through quarantine of all new fish is the only reliable protection.
Flukes (Gill and Skin)
Gyrodactylus, Dactylogyrus		 Scratching against surfaces; flared gills; increased mucus; gasping; loss of appetite; sometimes visible under magnification Salt baths (2–3% for short-term treatment); praziquantel (most effective and widely recommended treatment for flukes). Treat entire pond. Repeat after 10–14 days to address second generation. Microscopic examination of gill scraping confirms diagnosis.

Water Quality Is the Best Medicine

It bears repeating: healthy koi in clean, well-managed water are extraordinarily disease-resistant. Most disease outbreaks our team encounters in client ponds are directly traceable to an identifiable water quality failure — ammonia spike from overfeeding, pH crash from low KH, dissolved oxygen depletion during a heat wave, or filter crash from a power outage. Before reaching for any treatment, test the water. Fix the water. Then address the fish.

Koi Care in Southern California

Southern California koi keeping has some conditions that do not appear in most koi care literature, which is typically written for UK, Japanese, or northeastern US conditions. Understanding these SoCal-specific factors will make you a significantly more effective koi keeper.

Managing Summer Heat

The most critical summer consideration is water temperature. When a pond exceeds 82°F consistently — which can happen in inland Orange County, the San Fernando Valley, and Riverside County during heat waves — koi immune systems begin to weaken. Koi are cold-blooded animals: their metabolism, immune response, and disease susceptibility are all directly tied to water temperature. Above 86°F, koi are in physiological distress.

Practical summer heat management strategies:

  • Maximize aeration. Warm water holds less dissolved oxygen. Add an airstone or ensure your waterfall is running at maximum flow. On the hottest nights, aeration is life support.
  • Add shade. A shade sail or shade cloth over part of the pond surface can reduce surface temperature by 5 to 10 degrees. Even partial shading of 30 to 50 percent of the surface area makes a meaningful difference.
  • Stop feeding on extreme heat days. When air temperature exceeds 100°F or pond temperatures exceed 84°F, stop feeding entirely. Uneaten food will decompose rapidly, and koi digestion adds internal heat stress. Resume normal feeding when temperatures normalize.
  • Ice is an emergency measure only. Adding bagged ice to the pond can provide temporary relief during extreme heat events but is not a substitute for pond design that accommodates SoCal temperatures (adequate depth, shading, aeration).

Predator Management

The great blue heron (Ardea herodias) is the apex koi predator in coastal Southern California. These birds are large, patient, and remarkably effective at clearing shallow koi ponds overnight. They are also federally protected under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act — you cannot harm them. Effective deterrence strategies include:

  • Pond depth. A deep center zone (4+ feet) with steep sides gives koi a refuge that herons cannot wade into. This is the most reliable long-term solution.
  • Overhang or ledge. Herons approach ponds from the edge and wade in. A concrete or stone overhang at the pond edge (koi can hide underneath) eliminates the entry point.
  • Fishing line grid. Horizontal lines of monofilament fishing line at 6-inch intervals across the pond surface prevent herons from landing in the pond. Inexpensive but effective and invisible from most angles.
  • Motion-activated sprinklers. Scarecrow-style motion sprinklers are effective during the first weeks of heron activity at a new location, but herons eventually habituate to them.
  • Pond netting. Physical netting over the pond surface is the most reliable deterrent but the least aesthetically appealing. Used most commonly for ponds in areas with very high heron pressure.

Raccoons are the second major predator. They are opportunistic and will fish in shallow zones, but rarely venture into water deeper than 18 inches. Steep-sided, deep-center pond designs deter them naturally.

Year-Round Maintenance Schedule

Southern California koi keepers do not get a winter holiday. With koi remaining active and filtration running at full capacity 12 months per year, the maintenance schedule is essentially constant. Stick to your weekly and monthly tasks year-round. The only seasonal adjustments are feeding frequency (slightly reduced in cooler months) and feeding food type (wheat germ formula below 60°F). Everything else — water testing, filter checks, partial water changes, skimmer basket emptying — continues unchanged through December, January, and February.

UV Exposure and Koi Coloration

There is a silver lining to Southern California's intense sun: koi in SoCal ponds often develop exceptional coloration. Koi pigmentation — particularly the red (hi) and black (sumi) of Kohaku and Sanke — is intensified both by quality nutrition and by UV exposure. SoCal's abundant sunshine, combined with high-quality carotenoid-rich food (spirulina, krill), produces some of the most vividly colored koi you will see outside of Japan. Protect your fish from heat stress, but do not be in a rush to shade the pond entirely — moderate sun exposure is a genuine benefit for koi color development in our climate.
